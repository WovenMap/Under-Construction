design language · vol. 1 · the web · march 2030

Not a diagram. A living scene.

A weave is the picture a person carries in their head — the people, the projects, the promises, the way it all leans on itself. The job of the canvas is not to draw it. The job is to render it: with weight, light, depth, time, and the particular materiality of each kind of relationship.

What follows is the system. Atmosphere, things, threads, layers, time, interaction, moments — and one final scene that brings it all into one frame. We will not move on to the rest of the product until this is right.

I.
part one · the field the web sits in

Atmosphere, light, and the implied sun.

Before any node or connection, the canvas itself has weather. A direction of light. A horizon. A cool corner and a warm one. Things cast shadows because there is a sun.
layer 01

The paper.

A warm cream base. Subtle vertical gradient — a touch lighter at the top, a touch warmer at the bottom — and a very faint generative noise across the whole surface. Never pure white.

layer 02

The sun.

A directional warm light implied at upper-right, ~70°. Defines where shadows fall, where focus pools, where the eye lands first. Every node lit by this sun has a soft drop-shadow below-and-left.

layer 03

The far side.

Atmospheric perspective. The lower-left is cooler, denser, slightly desaturated — the air you'd push through to look at things further away. Distance has a colour in the same way altitude does.

layer 04

The horizon.

A barely-there ground line near the bottom of the canvas. Things further out into the page sit closer to it, and recede toward it. A piece of architecture, drawn once, used to organise everything.

PROOF · 01 · ATMOSPHERE COMPOSITED
no nodes · no connections · just the field

The dashboard era treated the canvas as a flat container — a uniform grey surface for shapes to live on. The 2030 instinct is to treat the canvas as a scene, with all the architectural decisions a scene requires.

Where is the light? Upper right, warm. Where is the cool? Lower left. Is there a horizon? Yes — implied, never drawn loud. Does the air get heavier toward the back of the picture? Yes. Is there grain in the paper? Always.

None of this is decoration. Every decision lets the things that come next — the nodes, the threads, the focus, the time — sit somewhere instead of nowhere.

II.
part two · what a thing is, in form

Six glyphs. Each one a character, not a shape.

Generic geometry tells the user "this is data." Custom glyphs tell the user "this is a person you know." Each thing-type has a small portrait.

Person

head + thread

A small portrait. Head, body in shadow, an upward attention-thread, two faint surround-marks for presence.

Project

folded card

A folded card with a turned corner. Suggests beginning, end, contained scope, a thing that can be opened.

Client

wax seal

A circular seal with a small mark inside. Suggests obligation, formality, a relationship that's been stamped.

Vendor

tag

A small label or tag, stacked, with a hole at the top. Suggests supplier, exchangeable, a thing that's catalogued.

Promise

knot

A small interlaced knot with a thread tail. Suggests "tied," "held," "until released" — the visual feel of a commitment.

Risk

flame

A small flame, burning. Distinctive at a glance — the only glyph in the system that uses rose-red. The eye finds risk first.

— Each glyph in five states. Demonstrated on Person.

default
hovered
focused · ambient pulse
archived · receded
draft · not yet confirmed

Each glyph is a small piece of illustration, designed once, used everywhere. They share a visual register — slightly hand-drawn, asymmetrical, weighted, with a soft drop-shadow that places them on the page.

Importantly, they're not interchangeable. The wax seal of a client says something different from the folded card of a project — and a brain that's read both for ten minutes will start to feel that difference. This is what makes the canvas readable at scale.

The five states are non-negotiable. Default, hovered, focused (the focal of the moment, with ambient pulse), archived (receded into the field), and draft (an inferred or unconfirmed thing the AI extracted but the user hasn't yet acknowledged).

III.
part three · the connective material

Six threads. Six materials.

A relationship between two people is not the same kind of relationship as a vendor contract. Why should they be drawn with the same line?

Edges in a graph are typically rendered uniformly. This is a missed opportunity. Different relationships have different physical qualities — and the canvas should show this. Below: six thread types, each with its own line quality, weight, and feel.

peer · friend · colleague
Silk

Soft, slightly wavy, opacity-feathered ends. The relationship between people who simply know each other.

contractual · obligation · funded
Rope

Twisted, weighty, two-strand visible. For commitments — money, deadlines, signed agreements. Heavier than it looks.

technical · dependency · pipeline
Wire

Taut, straight, with regular tick marks. For technical or systemic dependencies — code, data, infrastructure. Carries current.

inferred · suggested · unconfirmed
Mist

Dotted, faint, in muted ink. For relationships the system inferred but the user hasn't confirmed. An invitation to look.

monetary · pulse · funding flow
Vein

Swollen in the middle, tapered at the ends, in a quiet rose. For money flowing — a client funds a project funds a vendor. It pulses.

historical · derived · resolved
Echo

Three faint lines, fading down. For a relationship that was — closed, resolved, but worth remembering. The web's memory.

Each thread is rendered with a different SVG technique. Silk is a filled path with opacity gradient; rope is two interleaved sine waves; wire is a thin line with regular tick marks; mist is dashed in muted ink; vein is a tapered fill in rose; echo is three faded parallel lines.

The user doesn't need to learn a key. They feel the difference the way they feel the difference between a handshake and a signed contract.

Verb labels still exist — but they're set as small marginalia in mono, off the line, with a thin leader. The thread carries the weight of what kind of relationship; the verb carries the precision of which.

Silk filled path · opacity feather · soft wave Rope two sine waves · interlaced · weighted Wire thin line · regular ticks · taut Mist dashed · muted ink · 70% opacity Vein tapered rose · swelling middle Echo 3 parallel lines · fading down
A line is never just a line. It is the material of a relationship.
— guiding principle of part iii
IV.
part four · what is shown when

Three depths. Each one shows different things.

A web at thirty thousand feet is a shape. At ten thousand it's a structure. At ground level it's a conversation. The canvas should know which view you're in.
WIDE · 30,000 ft · the shape

The shape.

No labels. No verbs. Just dots and lines and the focal point. The eye reads the overall structure in two seconds — "there's the centre, there's the cluster, there's the outliers."

MID · 10,000 ft · the structure

The structure.

Glyphs become recognisable. The focal node has its name. Threads show their material. Periphery labels in small mono caps. Roles become visible.

CLOSE · ground level · the conversation

The conversation.

Full annotation. Names typeset in italic Fraunces. Roles in small caps. Verbs as marginalia. Status footnotes attached to specific things. The picture starts to read like a page.

Most products do this badly. They render everything visible at every zoom, then crowd-control with manual filtering. The 2030 instinct is the opposite: let the canvas decide what to show, based on how close the user is.

The handover between layers should be a tween, not a flicker. As the user zooms, glyphs grow, labels appear, marginalia fades in, threads reveal their material. The web breathes between scales.

Critically, the focal point and its closest neighbours always retain detail. If the user is looking at James, James never reduces to a dot — even at the wide view, his ring stays visible.

V.
part five · the breath of weeks

Time made visible, without a clock.

A thing touched five minutes ago should look different from a thing untouched for five months. The canvas should show this without saying it.

Just touched.

last 24 hours

A soft amber halo. The system noticed; the user can see.

Touched this week.

last 7 days

A small amber dot in the corner. A gentle "yes, this is alive."

Quiet.

7–30 days

No marker. Default. The thing sits in the field, present but unremarked.

Fading.

30+ days untouched

A slight blur. A drop in saturation. Still here, but receding into the field. An invitation to remember it.

The system already records when each thing was last modified. It shouldn't waste this signal. Time should be a permanent visible layer of the canvas.

The four states are deliberately understated. An amber dot, not a notification badge. A blur, not a strikethrough. The point is to give the eye a way to read the breath of the web — what's new, what's settling, what's quietly drifting away.

Pair this with ambient pulse on the focal node (2.6s loop, ~4% scale, ±50% opacity on the surrounding pool) and the canvas starts to feel continuously alive — not because anything is moving, but because the present is visibly present.

VI.
part six · the way a hand meets the canvas

The cursor, the bracket, the held thing.

An OS cursor on a designed canvas is a betrayal. The canvas should have its own mark — and the way it touches things should be designed.
state · cursor at rest

Cursor · at rest

Four small amber ticks describing a square — a subtle bracket. With a quiet mono mark beside it: a dot, a ∅, a question mark, depending on context. Never the OS arrow.

state · hover · bracket closes

Hover · bracket closes

The bracket grows, closes around the thing. A small ink-coloured tag flies to the upper right with name + type, connected by a hairline. Never a tooltip box with arrows. Never popovers.

state · selection · the held thing

Selection · the held thing

An amber ring + soft pool. The thing's shadow grows — visually, it has lifted from the page. A tiny mono caption underneath confirms what's held. Spacebar releases.

state · drag · threads stretch

Drag · the elastic move

A ghost remains where the thing came from. The held bracket shows where it's going. The connecting thread stretches in amber, like a pulled string. Release to commit; ESC to snap back.

state · voice · ambient listen

Voice · ambient listen

A small amber pulse in the lower corner says the canvas is listening. Spoken intent appears in italic Fraunces as it's transcribed. Below: a one-line interpretation in mono. The canvas is a quiet conversation partner.

state · inspect · margin opens

Inspect · the margin opens

Click-through (or two-finger tap) opens a margin. The page splits — canvas on the left, a typeset inspector panel on the right, separated by a single rule. Like opening a book.

The cursor is the most-touched object in any product. Most products use the OS one — and the design language stops at the cursor's edge. We do not.

The amber bracket carries through every interaction state: at rest as a small floating square, on hover as a closing frame around the focal thing, on drag as the held grip, on selection as the lock. One mark, used four ways, with a different label in mono each time.

By 2030, voice and gesture are not optional inputs — they're equal partners with mouse and keyboard. The canvas needs an ambient-listen state that's distinct from typing-mode, and the interface needs to be designed to accept partial, conversational instructions like add Priya as backup on Helix.

VII.
part seven · the canvas in transition

Loading. Empty. Drag-into. Re-thinking.

A canvas with nothing to show is also designed. An empty state is not a marketing poster — it's an invitation, in italics, with one thing to do.
LOADING · re-running the lenses

Loading.

A horizontal amber shimmer sweeps the canvas. Existing nodes mute slightly — they're being re-considered. A mono status line at the bottom names what's running and how long it's taking. Never a spinner.

EMPTY · before there is a web

Empty.

One seed. The user, alone in their field. An italic line: "It begins with you." A second, smaller: "Add the second thing." No buttons, no illustration of a person scratching their head. The simplest invitation.

DRAG-INTO · a document is incoming

Drag-into.

The canvas dims. An amber dashed bracket appears at the perimeter. A single italic line in the centre: "Drop the file." Below it, plain language explaining what happens next. The drop pool glows where to release.

State transitions are where most product design fails. The product looks great when it's full and broken when it's empty. Loading is a spinner. Empty is a stock illustration. Drag-into is a square with a plus in it.

The 2030 instinct is that every state earns a designed treatment. The empty state is a beat of poetry. The loading state is a small piece of choreography. The drag-into state is an invitation, not a UI affordance. None of them break the visual register the rest of the product is set in.

This is also where the canvas earns its trust. If the empty state feels considered, the user trusts the rest. If the empty state is a stock vector, they don't.

Loading horizontal shimmer · mono status · ~400ms Empty one seed · italic line · no buttons Drag-into amber dashed bracket · centred italic · drop pool Re-thinking existing nodes mute 30% while new ones tween in Error quiet rose tint at top · italic apology · the field stays
If the empty state is good, the rest of the product can be trusted.
— design law of part vii

part eight · the climactic rendering

All seven systems, in one frame.

Read across the frame. The warm light falls from the upper right; the far-field mist at far right hints at a wider web continuing beyond view; the amber compass notes the sun's position. Below the title, an editorial caption sets the moment.

This is not a network diagram. This is a small piece of editorial illustration that happens to be functional. The argument of the entire document, in one frame.

What we have committed to, and what's next.

The eight commitments

i. Atmosphere. The canvas is a scene, not a surface. Paper, sun, far-cool, horizon — composited every render.

ii. Things. Six custom glyphs in five states each. Designed once, used everywhere.

iii. Threads. Six materials. Different relationships, different physical qualities.

iv. Density layers. Wide / mid / close. The canvas adapts to how close the user is looking.

v. Time. Just-touched, this-week, quiet, fading. Visible without a clock.

vi. Interaction. Cursor, bracket, hover, selection, drag, voice, inspect. Designed objects, every one.

vii. Moments. Empty, loading, drag-into. Each state earns its own typesetting.

viii. The rendered web. All seven, in one frame. The argument made tangible.

What this implies for build

The Fabric isn't a react-flow with a custom theme. It's a custom rendering layer built on top of react-flow (or canvas/WebGL at scale), with our own node-renderer, edge-renderer, cursor-system, and atmospheric-overlay.

Reasonable framework: Pixi.js or react-three-fiber for the atmospheric layers (paper, sun, grain) with SVG composited on top for the glyphs and threads. Or, at smaller scale, all-SVG with the filters and gradients shown above.

One illustrator-designer commissioned to draw the six glyphs, each in five states, as a small system. Cost: ~2 weeks. Output: an SVG asset library that lives in the codebase and never has to be redrawn.

One motion-designer to specify the four loops (focal pulse, edge shimmer, drift, layer-tween). Cost: ~1 week. Output: documented timing curves.

The thread typology is the most ambitious system technically. Each thread material is a different SVG render. Worth prototyping all six up-front before committing.

Don't ship the voice mode in v1. Ship the listen affordance — the ambient-listen mark in the corner — even if it does nothing. It says "this is the kind of product that listens" from day one.

Where this language goes next

Once it lands here — on the canvas — it propagates. The dashboard uses the same atmospheric backdrop and pillar glyphs. The findings use the same typesetting and citations. The weekly digest emails use the same Newsreader body and Fraunces italic headlines. The brochure uses the same paper-cream and amber single accent.

Every screen we touch from here on inherits this language. Nothing else gets designed before this is in place.

What to refuse, all the way through

Don't render edges as straight lines. Even the wire has personality.

Don't use the OS cursor on the canvas. Hide it. The amber bracket is the cursor.

Don't use the rainbow palette for nodes. Ink is the canvas; rose marks risk; amber marks focus. That is the entire colour system.

Don't fill empty states with cartoons. A single italic line. A single seed. A single invitation.

Don't add bouncy motion. The canvas breathes. It does not perform.

Don't show numbers as headlines. Words first; shapes second; numbers third. Always in that order.

— this language is now locked. Any future visual work in the product checks back to this document. When the eight commitments hold, the design holds. When they begin to drift, the design has begun to drift. We will know what to fix.
